This page explains exactly how your bill is computed when you have reserved
capacity. Read it once carefully вАФ there are very few surprises, but the
ones that exist are worth understanding before you commit dollars.

## The one rule

> **At every instant within an interval, your reserved capacity acts as a
> flat ceiling. Memory at or below the ceiling is covered by your
> reservation. Memory above the ceiling is overage.**

That's the entire model. Everything else on this page is restating, working
through examples, and pointing out the edge cases.

## Said three ways

Three different framings of the same rule. Pick whichever clicks for you.

**Framing 1 вАФ Ceiling.** Imagine a horizontal line drawn at your
`reservedGb` level on a chart of your concurrent memory over time. Anything
the chart pokes above that line is overage; anything below is covered.

**Framing 2 вАФ Per-second.** At every second `t` within an interval:
- if `usage(t) вЙ§ reservedGb`: that second is covered by your reservation
- if `usage(t) > reservedGb`: the difference contributes to overage for
  that second

**Framing 3 вАФ Formula.** For each 15-minute interval, per `(org, resource)`:

```text
reservedBilled  =  reservedGb √Ч 900 sec                    (full commitment)
overageBilled   =  вИС_t  max(usage(t) вИТ reservedGb, 0)       (per-second integration)
```

## A complete worked example

You reserve `16 GB` for the interval `02:00вАУ02:15` (900 seconds). During
the interval your sandboxes use:

| Phase | Duration | Concurrent memory |
| ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 1 | 600 s | 8 GB |
| 2 | 60 s | 30 GB |
| 3 | 240 s | 0 GB |

### Step 1 вАФ split each phase into reserved vs overage

For each phase, apply the rule "min for reserved, max-minus-reserved for
overage."

| Phase | Memory | Reserved component (per second) | Overage component (per second) |
| ---: | ---: | --- | --- |
| 1 | 8 GB | `min(8, 16) = 8 GB`  | `max(8 вИТ 16, 0) = 0 GB`  |
| 2 | 30 GB | `min(30, 16) = 16 GB` | `max(30 вИТ 16, 0) = 14 GB` |
| 3 | 0 GB | `min(0, 16) = 0 GB`  | `max(0 вИТ 16, 0) = 0 GB`  |

### Step 2 вАФ multiply by duration to get GB-seconds

| Phase | Reserved (GB ¬Ј s) | Overage (GB ¬Ј s) |
| ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 1 | 8 √Ч 600 = **4 800** | 0 √Ч 600 = **0** |
| 2 | 16 √Ч 60 = **960** | 14 √Ч 60 = **840** |
| 3 | 0 √Ч 240 = **0** | 0 √Ч 240 = **0** |
| **Sum** | **5 760 GB-s actual reserved consumption** | **840 GB-s overage** |

Notice: of the 14 400 GB-seconds you reserved (16 √Ч 900), you actually
*used* 5 760 GB-seconds of it. The remaining 8 640 GB-seconds went unused.

### Step 3 вАФ bill the commitment, not the consumption

Here's the critical point. Reserved capacity is billed in full,
regardless of how much of it you actually consumed. Your overage is
billed separately.

| Charge | GB-seconds | Rate |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Reserved (commitment, always full) | 16 √Ч 900 = **14 400** | reserved-tier rate |
| Overage (per-second spike) | **840** | overage-tier rate |

You pay for the **full 14 400 GB-seconds** at the reserved rate, plus
**840 GB-seconds** at the overage rate. The 8 640 unused GB-seconds are
still billed вАФ that's what "commitment" means.

## Three more examples to nail it down

The model deserves repetition.

### Example A вАФ usage stays below reserved the whole time

You reserve 16 GB, average usage is 4 GB the whole interval.

| | Reserved (GB ¬Ј s) | Overage (GB ¬Ј s) |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| Per second | min(4, 16) = 4 | max(4 вИТ 16, 0) = 0 |
| Over 900 s | 3 600 actual consumption | **0 overage** |

Bill:
- Reserved (commitment): **16 √Ч 900 = 14 400 GB-s** at reserved rate.
- Overage: **0**.

You paid for 14 400 GB-seconds of reservation. You only used 3 600. No
overage because you stayed below the ceiling. The unused 10 800 is the
cost of the commitment.

### Example B вАФ usage stays above reserved the whole time

You reserve 16 GB, sandbox runs at 24 GB the whole interval.

| | Reserved (GB ¬Ј s) | Overage (GB ¬Ј s) |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| Per second | min(24, 16) = 16 | max(24 вИТ 16, 0) = 8 |
| Over 900 s | 14 400 covered | **7 200 overage** |

Bill:
- Reserved (commitment): **14 400 GB-s** at reserved rate.
- Overage: **7 200 GB-s** at overage rate.

The reservation absorbed 16 GB at every instant. The 8 GB above the
ceiling at every instant became overage. You ran 24 √Ч 900 = 21 600 GB-s
of compute total; 14 400 is covered, 7 200 is overage.

### Example C вАФ usage averages below reserved but briefly spikes above

You reserve 16 GB. Usage is 4 GB for 800 seconds, then 25 GB for 100
seconds.

| Phase | Memory | Reserved per second | Overage per second |
| ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| 800 s | 4 GB | 4 | 0 |
| 100 s | 25 GB | 16 | 9 |

Aggregated:

| | Reserved (GB ¬Ј s) | Overage (GB ¬Ј s) |
| ---: | ---: | ---: |
| First 800 s | 3 200 | 0 |
| Last 100 s | 1 600 | **900** |
| **Sum** | **4 800 actual consumption** | **900 overage** |

Bill:
- Reserved (commitment): **14 400 GB-s** at reserved rate.
- Overage: **900 GB-s** at overage rate.

**This is the crucial counterintuitive case.** Your average usage was
`(4 √Ч 800 + 25 √Ч 100) / 900 вЙИ 6.8 GB` вАФ well under your 16 GB reservation.
You might think "I didn't use up my reservation, so there should be no
overage." That's wrong.

The reservation is a flat ceiling **at every instant**, not a budget
averaged over the interval. The 100-second spike above the ceiling
produces 900 GB-seconds of overage during those 100 seconds, regardless
of what happened in the other 800 seconds.

If this matters to you, the lever is to reserve enough to cover your
peaks, not your average.

## The two charges are independent

It bears emphasizing again because it's where most confusion comes from.

| Question | Answer |
| --- | --- |
| Does my reservation amount affect my reserved bill? | Yes. Reserved bill = `reservedGb √Ч 900` per interval, always. |
| Does my actual usage affect my reserved bill? | **No.** You pay the full reservation regardless. |
| Does my reservation amount affect my overage bill? | Yes. Higher reserved ceiling = less spike sticking out above it = less overage. |
| Does my actual usage affect my overage bill? | Yes. Overage = sum of seconds where usage exceeded the ceiling. |
| If I use less than my reservation, do I get a credit? | **No.** Unused reserved capacity is the cost of commitment. |
| If I use exactly my reservation, do I pay overage? | No. At every instant, usage is at-or-below the ceiling. |

The reserved charge is the price of optionality you gave up. The overage
charge is the price of optionality you kept (the spikes above your
ceiling).

## Common misconceptions, explicitly addressed

We're going to walk through phrases you might think to yourself or hear
from a teammate. For each, the answer.

> **"I have 16 GB reserved. I'll only get charged overage if my total
> usage in this interval exceeds 16 √Ч 900 = 14 400 GB-seconds."**

No. Reserved capacity is not a GB-second budget. It is a flat ceiling at
every instant. A 60-second spike to 30 GB produces overage during those
60 seconds, even if your average is below the ceiling.

> **"I averaged 8 GB across the interval, my reservation is 16 GB, so
> there can't be any overage."**

Wrong if you ever spiked above 16 GB. The averaging argument doesn't
apply вАФ every second is settled independently against the ceiling.

> **"I never used my reservation; surely I shouldn't pay for it."**

You pay for it because that's what reserving means. The discount on the
reserved rate exists *because* you committed unconditionally. If
unused-but-paid behavior bothers you, reserve less and let on-demand
absorb the variability.

> **"My reservation should auto-resize to cover spikes."**

It doesn't. The reservation is whatever you committed to. Spikes above
the ceiling become overage. If you want spikes covered, reserve a higher
amount.

> **"If I cancel a sandbox in the middle of the interval, my unused
> reservation should roll over to the next interval."**

It doesn't. Reservations are per-interval; unused capacity in one
interval doesn't flow to the next.

> **"Reserved capacity makes the platform run my sandboxes faster /
> guarantee they start."**

Reserved capacity doesn't change runtime behavior. It guarantees the
platform has set aside capacity for you in that interval, so you won't
be admission-blocked. The sandboxes themselves run the same way.

## Edge cases at a glance

| Case | Behavior |
| --- | --- |
| You hold reservations but run nothing | Reserved capacity billed in full (commitment). No overage. |
| You run workloads but have no reservations | `reservedGb = 0`, so the ceiling is at zero. **Every second of usage is overage.** Same as today's pure on-demand bill. |
| You hold reservations in one interval and run in a different one | No carryover. Allocation is strictly per interval. |
| You run multiple sandboxes whose memory sums above your reservation | Their concurrent memory is summed, then the ceiling rule applies. No per-sandbox attribution вАФ the platform doesn't decide which sandbox is "the reserved one." |
| Your reservation matches your spike but not your average | You pay the full reservation (committed at the spike size); spikes are covered; below-spike time is below the ceiling and consumed at the reserved rate. Sometimes the right shape вАФ covers worst-case at the lower rate. |
| A sandbox crashes mid-interval | Memory drops as soon as the scale event closes. The per-second integration sees the new lower memory immediately for subsequent seconds. |
| You make a reservation mid-interval | Not possible вАФ reservations must be made before the interval starts (`earliestReservableStart` enforces a lead time). The interval you're already in is on-demand only. |

## Why per-second instead of aggregate?

It's simpler and matches the "capacity available" intuition.

If we used a GB-second budget вАФ "you have 14 400 GB-seconds budgeted
for this interval, you only pay overage when you exceed that total" вАФ
then a brief spike of arbitrary magnitude could be free as long as it's
balanced by quiet time. That makes reservations behave like burstable
budgets, which is harder to reason about and creates incentives for
adversarial spike patterns.

Per-second settlement is honest: at every moment, your committed
capacity is what you committed to. Above it is overage. Below it is
covered. No gaming, no averaging, no edge cases.
